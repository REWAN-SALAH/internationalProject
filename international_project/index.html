Gender Inequality and Feminism in Turkey

Gender inequality remains a deeply rooted and pressing issue in Turkey, affecting millions of women in various aspects of their lives. Women face systemic challenges in accessing equal rights, such as widespread discrimination in workplaces, domestic violence, and societal norms that limit their opportunities for education, employment, and leadership roles. For example, many women are often paid less than men for the same work or are excluded from decision-making positions due to entrenched gender biases.

Despite constitutional reforms that promise equality, patriarchal cultural norms and societal expectations often prevent their effective implementation. For instance, early and forced marriages continue to be a practice in some rural areas, depriving young girls of education and leading to lifelong socio-economic disadvantages. Additionally, laws aimed at protecting women from violence, such as the Istanbul Convention, face resistance and insufficient enforcement in parts of the country.

Feminist movements in Turkey have been instrumental in addressing these issues, organizing protests and campaigns to bring attention to domestic violence, child marriages , and wage inequality. Notable examples include the protests against femicide, where women march with banners and demand justice for victims of gender-based violence. Activists have also worked tirelessly to challenge outdated legal practices and advocate for the creation of shelters and support systems for survivors of abuse.

As feminist group rally for their rights, they emphasize the need for education as a transformative tool to challenge gender stereotypes and empower young girls. They also push for comprehensive legal reforms that address loopholes in existing policies and demand international cooperation to ensure that global standards for women's rights are upheld. Their fight is not just about achieving equality for women but also about building a fairer, more inclusive society where all individuals can thrive regardless of gender.

Key Challenges

Abusive behaviors in Turkey predominantly affect women, with domestic violence being a significant concern. According to the "Statistical Analysis Report of Domestic Violence Emergency Hotline Data for the Years 2007-2021," 90% of perpetrators are men, with 63% being spouses and 21% family members. The report also indicates that 8 in 10 individuals subjected to violence are women, highlighting the pervasive nature of abuse within familial settings.

Dysfunctional societal norms in Turkey often perpetuate gender inequality. Traditional gender roles assign women the primary responsibility for household chores and caregiving, limiting their participation in the workforce. The labor force participation rate for women is 32%, compared to 68% for men, reflecting these entrenched societal expectations.

Manipulative practices in Turkey include societal pressures that enforce traditional gender roles, restricting women's autonomy. For instance, women may face coercion to conform to expectations regarding marriage and family life, limiting their personal and professional choices. Such manipulation reinforces patriarchal structures and hinders progress toward gender equality.

Oppressive policies and practices in Turkey have undermined women's rights. The country's withdrawal from the Istanbul Convention in 2021, a treaty aimed at preventing violence against women, marked a significant setback. This decision has been criticized for exposing women to greater risks of violence and discrimination.

Women in Turkey advocating for their rights often face persecution, including legal challenges and societal backlash. For example, women's rights activists have been detained during protests, such as those marking the International Day for the Elimination of Violence Against Women. These actions reflect the challenges women encounter when seeking to promote gender equality.

Physical assaults against women remain a critical issue in Turkey. Reports indicate that over 400 women were murdered in 2024 alone, underscoring the severity of femicide in the country. Despite legal frameworks intended to protect women, enforcement remains inconsistent, leaving many vulnerable to violence.

The legal system's failure to protect women effectively often results in their victimization. Human Rights Watch has documented cases where authorities did not enforce court orders meant to protect women from domestic violence, leading to continued abuse and, in some instances, death.

Gender discrimination in Turkey is evident in various sectors, including employment and education. The unemployment rate for women is 14.5%, slightly higher than the 13% rate for men, reflecting disparities in economic opportunities. Additionally, societal norms often discourage women from pursuing higher education or careers, limiting their professional advancement.

Inhumane practices, such as forced marriages and honor killings, persist in some regions of Turkey. For example, a British woman was tricked into traveling to Turkey, where she was forcibly married and subjected to years of abuse. Such cases highlight the severe human rights violations that women can face.

Why This Matters

More than one in three Turkish women has been subjected to domestic violence. Policies like forced marriages and mutilation still exist in some areas. It's time for change.
